    THE POWER GOOD SIGNAL

    The Power_Good signal is sometimes called the Power_OK or PWR_OK is a +5V (normal) active high signal (with a variation from +2.4V through +6.0V generally being considered satisfactory) that is supplied to the motherboard when the power supply has passed its internal self-tests and the output voltage have stabilized.

    This typically takes anywhere from 100ms to 500ms (0.1-0.5 seconds) after you turn on the power supply switch. It then sends the PWR_OK signal to the motherboard where the processor timer chip that controls the rest line to the processor receives it. In the absence of PWR_OK, the timer chip holds the reset line on the processor, which prevents the system from running under bad or unstable power conditions. When the timer chip receives the PWR_OK signal it releases the rest and the processor beings

    Magnetic Storage Principles

    Most permeant or semi-permanent computer data is stored magnetically, meaning a stream of binary computer data bits (0’s and 1’s) is stored by magnetizing tiny pieces of metal embedded on the surface of a disk or tape in a pattern that represents data. Later, this magnetic pattern can be read and converted back into the same original bits. This is the principle of magnetic storage and the overall chapter.

    History

    The history starts in June 1949, when a team of engineers and scientists began working on a new storage device. That storage device was the first magnetic storage device for computers and completely changed the industry. May 21, 1952, IBM released the IBM 726 Tape Unit with the IBM701 Defense Calculator, showing the shift from punch card calculators to electronic computers. In September 13, 1956 another group in San Jose, Cali, announced the first computer disk storage system as part of the 305 RAMAC (Random Access Method of Accounting and Control) computer. The RAMAC could only hold 5MB of data on 50 disks, with a size of 24 inches in diameter. This random accessibility changed the effect of computer performance at that point, allowing to be stored and retrieved considerably faster than if it were on a tape. In just over 60+ years the magnetic storage industry has moved quickly, now you can store 4TB or more in a 3 ½ inch drives that fit in to a single computer drive bay.

    Magnetic Fields and How They are Used

    All magnetic storage device is able to read and write data by electromagnetism. The basic idea behind it is that the physics state that of an electric current goes through a wire and a magnetic field is generated around the conductor. Keep in mind the electrons actually move from negative to positive, while we normally think they go in the other direction. Electromagnetism started in 1819 by a Danish physicist Hans Christian Oersted, when he found that a compass needle would deflect away from pointing north when brought close to a wire that held an electric current running through it. After the electricity was cut off, the needle went back to using the Earth’s magnetic field and was at north again. Another effect of electromagnetism was found by Michael Faraday in 1831. He discovered that if a conductor is passed through a moving magnetic field, a current was created. As the polarity of the magnetic field changes so does the current’s flows direction. The term flux is used to describe a magnetic field that has a specific direction or polarity. At the top of the surface, the medium moves under the drive head, which can generate something called a magnetic flux of a given polarity over a specific region of the medium. When the flow is flipped, the coils in the head is also flipped, so the magnetic field polarity or flux in the head gap. A signal when reading 0 volts unless the head detects a magnetic flux transition, in which it generates a positive or negative pulse accordingly. There has been several types of heads that have been used:
  • ۝ Ferrite
  • ۝ Metal-In-Gap (MIG)
  • ۝ Thin-Film (TF)
  • ۝ Magneto-resistive (MR)
  • ۝ Giant magneto-resistive (GMR)

    Ferrite

    Ferrite heads, the original type used and based off of IBM 30-30 Winchester drive. These types have an iron-oxide core wrapped in electromagnetic coils. The drive generates a magnetic field by energizing the coils or passing a magnetic field neat them. This allows them to have a full read//write capability. Ferrite heads are large and weighs more than the thin-film heads and because of that they require a larger floating height to prevent contact with the disk while it is rotating.

    Metal-In-Gap

    MIG heads are purposely enhanced version of the composite ferrite design. In MIG heads, a metal substance is applied to the head’s recording gap. Two versions of MIF heads are available, single sided and double sided. Single sided MIG heads are designed with a layer of magnetic allot placed amount the trailing edge of the gap. Double sided MIG designs apply the lay to both sides of the gap. The metal alloy is applied through a vacuum deposition process called sputtering.

    Thin-Film

    Thin-Film heads are manufactured much the same way as a semiconductor chip- through a photolithographic processes. This process creates many thousands of heads in a single circular wafer and produces a small, high-quality product.

    Magneto-resistive

    Magneto-resistive heads, sometimes called as the anisotropic magneto resistant (AMR) heads, are capable of increasing density four times or greater as compared to the previous inductive only heads. IBM introduced the first commercially available drive with MR heads in 1991, in a 1GB 3 ½ inch model, and others quickly followed suit. MR heads are more costly and complex to make then the older TF heads because of these additional components and manufacture steps required: Additional wires must be run to and from the head to carry the sense current. Four to six more masking steps are required. Because of the MR heads being so sensitive, they are susceptible to stray magnetic fields and have to have additional shielding.

    Giant Magneto Resistive

    When working on a way to get more density, IBM announced a new type of head in 1997. Called the Giant Magneto Resistive heads, they are smaller than standard MR heads but are named for the GMR effect on which they are based. The design, while similar, has additional layers replaced the single NiFe layer in the conventional MR design. In MR heads, a single NiFe film changes resistance in response to a flux reversal on the disk. In GMR heads, two films (disconnected by a thin copper conducting layer) performed this function. The GMR mentioned previously was first discovered in 1988, in crystal samples that were exposed to high-power magnetic fields (1,000 times more used in hard disk drives). Two scientist named Peter Gruenberg of Julich, Germany, and Albert Fert of Paris discovered that large resistance changes were occurring in materials composed of alternating thin layers of various metallic elements. The important part of GMR is that a layer of nonmagnetic metal lays between two layers of magnetic metals. One of the layers is pinned, meaning that ut has a forced magnetic orientation. The other is free, meaning that it is free to move orientation or alignment. IBM revealed the first commercially available drive using GMR heads (a 16.8GB 3 ½-inch drive) in December 1997. Ever since then, GMR heads are standard in most 3.5 inch and 2.5 inch drives. 2007 saw Hitachi develop a giant magneto-resistive head using perpendicular current that supports areal densities of up to 1Tbits/sq. inch or more. These are called current perpendicular-to-the-plane giant magneto-resistive beads or CPP-GMR for short, and started appearing on the market around 2011.

    Definition of a Hard Disk

    The hard disk drive (HDD) is a highly important but mystifying part of a computer system. HDDs are sealed units used for nonvolatile storage. Nonvolatile or semi permanent storage means that a storage device keeps the data with no power supply. Because HDDs store crucial programming and data, the consequences of failures are usually serious. To build, maintain, service, or upgrade a PC system properly, it is important to know how hard disk functions. HDDs hold rigid, circular platters, usually constructed of aluminum or glass. These platters cannot be flexed or bent, thus the term hard disk. In most drives you can’t remove the platters, which is why someone may refer to them as fixed disk drives.

    History of Hard Drives

    The first ever hard drive appeared in 1957, Cyril Northcote Parkinson published his famous compilation of essays called Parkinson’s Law, which begins with the words, “Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.” A corollary of Parkinson’s most famous “law” can be applied to hard drives: “Data expands so as to fill the space available for its storage.” This, obviously, means that it doesn’t matter how large of a hard drive you have, you will fill it. Today, we have hard drive disks that have a rate of 550 MBps or more, looking back that is more than 1,000 to 5, 5000 faster. Up until the advent of high-performance Solid-State Drives (SSDs) these interface were typically much faster than the individual drives they supported, meaning that the true transfer rate you will see was almost entirely limited by the driver and not the interface you choose. SSDs have changed the system and have been pushing the industry to develop faster interfaces. The modern interfaces have bandwidth to spare for future developments and advances in hard disk technology.

    Form Factors

    The PC industry has always been about standards, with disk drives, it is evident that in the physical and electrical form factors that comprise modern drives. By using this standard, you can purchase a system or chassis from one company and yet have the physical and electrical components come from a different manufacture. Form Factors ensure that available drives will fit in the bay, the screw holes will line up, and the standard cables and connections will plug in. Form Factors are established in a few different ways. A few are simply because of high popularity, and others copy the form. In other cases there various groups or committees have been formed to dctate3 certain industry standards. It is really up to the companies that make applicable products to create them to conform to these standards.

    5 12 Inch Drive

    Shugart introduced the 5 ¼ floppy drives along with the first 5 ¼ form factor in 1976. Seagate released the first 5 ¼ hard disk in 1980, predating IBM PC. IBM later used the Seagate ST-412 (10MB) drive in some of its PC-XT models, which were among the first PCs to be sold with hard drives built in. The first portable systems (such as the original Compaq Portable) used these drives as well. Later, the 5 ¼ inch half height floppy drive and hard drives were introduced. This allowed two drives to fit in a bay originally designed for one. The 5 ¼ half-height factor is still used today as the form factor for optical drives and is the standard for large drive bays in all modern desktop PC chassis.

    3 ½ Inch Drive

    Sony actually was the one to first introduce the 3 ½ inch floppy drive in 1981, which used a smaller width and depth but had the same height as the 5 ¼ inch. These were called 3 ½ inch half height drives; even though there is no such thing as a full height 3 ½ drive. Rodime followed with the first 3 ½ half height hard disk in 1983. Afterwards, the 3 ½ floppy and hard drive would be reduced to only 1 inch which was just under the one-third original of 5 ¼ form factor (these were referred to by some as the 1/3 height drives). Today, the 1-inch version has become modern industry standard 3 ½ form factor.

    2 ½ Inch Drive

Despite PairieTek being the first with that form factor, other drive manufactures quickly capitalized on the market by also bringing in their 2 ½ drives. In 1994 Conner Peripherals, Inc., paid $18 million for PairieTek’s drive, and they went out of business. Although the 2 ½ can be used for desktop computers, the 3 ½ inch drive still tops the market due to greater speed and capacity as well as lower cost.     1.8 Inch Drive

    The 1.8 inch drive was first introduced in 1991, but it never really became popular. The main reason for making the 1.8 was to fit perfectly in the PC Card (PCMCIA) form factor, making it ideal for add on removable storage for laptop and notebook systems. The main reason why it never really took to the market was because it was slow to take shape and in 1998 an investment group called Mobile Storage bought Integral Peripherals (the company that made the product). There has been several companies that have made the 1.8, such as HP, Calluna, Toshiba, and Hitachi. HP exited the drive market completely in 1996, and Calluna creased operation in 2001. Toshiba released its 1.8 (which is available in the physical format of a Type II PC Card in 2000, and Hitachi entered the 1.8 market in 2003. Toshiba announced its last 1.8 drives in capacities of up to 320GB in 2009. There are no 1.8 HDDs currently being made, but they are still available in marketplace for repair and several manufactures are making SSDs in the 1.8 form factor.

    I-Inch drive

    1998 saw IBM releasing a 1-inch drive called the Microdrive, incorporating a single platter about the size of a quarter. Seagates introduced a similar device in 2004. Microdrive capacities varied from 170MB to 12GB over the life of these products. The drives were available with several interfaces but the most popular was that of a Type 2 Compact Flash (CF) card. This means that the drives could be used in almost any device that took CF cards, including digital cameras, PDAs (personal digital assistants) and MP3 players such as the Apple iPod Mini. IBM’s drive division was sold to Hitachi in 2003 and combined with Hitachi’s storage technology as Hitachi Global Storage Technologies. Hitachi and Seagate shifted out Microdive production after 2008 since by then flash-based CF cards had surpassed the Microdrive in capacity, performance, and price. In 2004, Toshiba released the smallest drive to date: the 0.85-inch drive, which is about the size of a postage stamp and stores up to 4GB. This drive was designed for use in mobile phones, digital audio players, PDAs, digital still cameras, camcorders, and more. As with the Microdrive, these were also eventually stopped being made due to advances in flash memory.
