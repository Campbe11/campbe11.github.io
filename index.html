    Magnetic Storage Principles

    Most permeant or semi-permanent computer data is stored magnetically, meaning a stream of binary computer data bits (0’s and 1’s) is stored by magnetizing tiny pieces of metal embedded on the surface of a disk or tape in a pattern that represents data. Later, this magnetic pattern can be read and converted back into the same original bits. This is the principle of magnetic storage and the overall chapter.

    History

    The history starts in June 1949, when a team of engineers and scientists began working on a new storage device. That storage device was the first magnetic storage device for computers and completely changed the industry. May 21, 1952, IBM released the IBM 726 Tape Unit with the IBM701 Defense Calculator, showing the shift from punch card calculators to electronic computers. In September 13, 1956 another group in San Jose, Cali, announced the first computer disk storage system as part of the 305 RAMAC (Random Access Method of Accounting and Control) computer. The RAMAC could only hold 5MB of data on 50 disks, with a size of 24 inches in diameter. This random accessibility changed the effect of computer performance at that point, allowing to be stored and retrieved considerably faster than if it were on a tape. In just over 60+ years the magnetic storage industry has moved quickly, now you can store 4TB or more in a 3 ½ inch drives that fit in to a single computer drive bay.

    Magnetic Fields and How They are Used

    All magnetic storage device is able to read and write data by electromagnetism. The basic idea behind it is that the physics state that of an electric current goes through a wire and a magnetic field is generated around the conductor. Keep in mind the electrons actually move from negative to positive, while we normally think they go in the other direction. Electromagnetism started in 1819 by a Danish physicist Hans Christian Oersted, when he found that a compass needle would deflect away from pointing north when brought close to a wire that held an electric current running through it. After the electricity was cut off, the needle went back to using the Earth’s magnetic field and was at north again. Another effect of electromagnetism was found by Michael Faraday in 1831. He discovered that if a conductor is passed through a moving magnetic field, a current was created. As the polarity of the magnetic field changes so does the current’s flows direction. The term flux is used to describe a magnetic field that has a specific direction or polarity. At the top of the surface, the medium moves under the drive head, which can generate something called a magnetic flux of a given polarity over a specific region of the medium. When the flow is flipped, the coils in the head is also flipped, so the magnetic field polarity or flux in the head gap. A signal when reading 0 volts unless the head detects a magnetic flux transition, in which it generates a positive or negative pulse accordingly. There has been several types of heads that have been used:
  • ۝ Ferrite
  • ۝ Metal-In-Gap (MIG)
  • ۝ Thin-Film (TF)
  • ۝ Magneto-resistive (MR)

    Ferrite

    Ferrite heads, the original type used and based off of IBM 30-30 Winchester drive. These types have an iron-oxide core wrapped in electromagnetic coils. The drive generates a magnetic field by energizing the coils or passing a magnetic field neat them. This allows them to have a full read//write capability. Ferrite heads are large and weighs more than the thin-film heads and because of that they require a larger floating height to prevent contact with the disk while it is rotating.

    Metal-In-Gap

    MIG heads are purposely enhanced version of the composite ferrite design. In MIG heads, a metal substance is applied to the head’s recording gap. Two versions of MIF heads are available, single sided and double sided. Single sided MIG heads are designed with a layer of magnetic allot placed amount the trailing edge of the gap. Double sided MIG designs apply the lay to both sides of the gap. The metal alloy is applied through a vacuum deposition process called sputtering.

    Thin-Film

    Thin-Film heads are manufactured much the same way as a semiconductor chip- through a photolithographic processes. This process creates many thousands of heads in a single circular wafer and produces a small, high-quality product.

    Magneto-resistive

    Magneto-resistive heads, sometimes called as the anisotropic magneto resistant (AMR) heads, are capable of increasing density four times or greater as compared to the previous inductive only heads. IBM introduced the first commercially available drive with MR heads in 1991, in a 1GB 3 ½ inch model, and others quickly followed suit. MR heads are more costly and complex to make then the older TF heads because of these additional components and manufacture steps required: Additional wires must be run to and from the head to carry the sense current. Four to six more masking steps are required. Because of the MR heads being so sensitive, they are susceptible to stray magnetic fields and have to have additional shielding.

    Giant Magneto Resistive

    When working on a way to get more density, IBM announced a new type of head in 1997. Called the Giant Magneto Resistive heads, they are smaller than standard MR heads but are named for the GMR effect on which they are based. The design, while similar, has additional layers replaced the single NiFe layer in the conventional MR design. In MR heads, a single NiFe film changes resistance in response to a flux reversal on the disk. In GMR heads, two films (disconnected by a thin copper conducting layer) performed this function. The GMR mentioned previously was first discovered in 1988, in crystal samples that were exposed to high-power magnetic fields (1,000 times more used in hard disk drives). Two scientist named Peter Gruenberg of Julich, Germany, and Albert Fert of Paris discovered that large resistance changes were occurring in materials composed of alternating thin layers of various metallic elements. The important part of GMR is that a layer of nonmagnetic metal lays between two layers of magnetic metals. One of the layers is pinned, meaning that ut has a forced magnetic orientation. The other is free, meaning that it is free to move orientation or alignment. IBM revealed the first commercially available drive using GMR heads (a 16.8GB 3 ½-inch drive) in December 1997. Ever since then, GMR heads are standard in most 3.5 inch and 2.5 inch drives. 2007 saw Hitachi develop a giant magneto-resistive head using perpendicular current that supports areal densities of up to 1Tbits/sq. inch or more. These are called current perpendicular-to-the-plane giant magneto-resistive beads or CPP-GMR for short, and started appearing on the market around 2011.

    Definition of a Hard Disk

    The hard disk drive (HDD) is a highly important but mystifying part of a computer system. HDDs are sealed units used for nonvolatile storage. Nonvolatile or semi permanent storage means that a storage device keeps the data with no power supply. Because HDDs store crucial programming and data, the consequences of failures are usually serious. To build, maintain, service, or upgrade a PC system properly, it is important to know how hard disk functions. HDDs hold rigid, circular platters, usually constructed of aluminum or glass. These platters cannot be flexed or bent, thus the term hard disk. In most drives you can’t remove the platters, which is why someone may refer to them as fixed disk drives.

    History of Hard Drives

    The first ever hard drive appeared in 1957, Cyril Northcote Parkinson published his famous compilation of essays called Parkinson’s Law, which begins with the words, “Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.” A corollary of Parkinson’s most famous “law” can be applied to hard drives: “Data expands so as to fill the space available for its storage.” This, obviously, means that it doesn’t matter how large of a hard drive you have, you will fill it. Today, we have hard drive disks that have a rate of 550 MBps or more, looking back that is more than 1,000 to 5, 5000 faster. Up until the advent of high-performance Solid-State Drives (SSDs) these interface were typically much faster than the individual drives they supported, meaning that the true transfer rate you will see was almost entirely limited by the driver and not the interface you choose. SSDs have changed the system and have been pushing the industry to develop faster interfaces. The modern interfaces have bandwidth to spare for future developments and advances in hard disk technology.

Flash Memory Devices Flash memory is a special type of solid state memory chip that requires no power to maintain its contents. Flash memory was invented by Fujio Masuoka at Toshiba in the early 1980s, with the original patterns filled in later 1981.At the time Toshiba didn’t know how important this invention was, and by 1988 Intel had introduced competitive versions and quickly took the lead in flash memory development and production. Flash memory is a type of nonvolatile memory that can be electrically programmed and erased. It was originally used in PC motherboards such as EEPROM (electronically erasable programmable read-only memory) chips for storing the motherboard basic input/output system (BIOS). Flash took over for EPROM which can only be programmed or erased using special equipment outside of the motherboard. Most consider Flash Memory to be a cross between RAM and ROM. Just like RAM flash can be written directly in the system, but it is nonvolatile. When erased flash memory cells are in a low-voltage state that holds a logical value of 1. The act of programming flash places a charge in the transistor’s floating gate which changed the 1 to a 0. The problem with that is that you can only program single cells or pages, you can only erase only on a block basis, and a black usually consist of electrons into and out of the transistor’s floating gate by a process known as the Fowler-Nordheim tunneling. Three are two big types of flash memory, called NOR (Not OR) and NAND (Not AND). The have the same basic transistor design but they differ in how they interconnected. Nor flash works more like dynamic RAM and NOR is more like the flash memory for flash ROMs. NAND works more like a storage device and NAND is denser then NOR.     CompactFlash

    Compact Flash was developed by SanDisk Corporation in 1994 and uses the ATA (AT attachment) architecture to emulate a disk drive; a CompactFlash device attached to a computer has a disk drive letter just like your other drives. When CompactFlash was first being standardized, even full-sized hard disk was rarely over 4GB, so the limitations of the ATA standard was considered acceptable. However, CF cards manufactured after the original Revision 1.0 specification are available in capacities up to 128GiB. While the current revision 6.0 works in [P]ATA mode, future revisions are expected to implement in SATA mode. The original size was Type 1 (3.3mm thick) a new Type 2 (5mm thick) accommodates higher capacity devices. Both CompactFlash cards are 1.433 inch wide by 1.685 inch long, and adapters allow them to be inserted into laptop computer PC Card slots. The CompactFlash Association oversees development of the standard.

    SmartMedia

    Despite the name, SmartMedia is actually the simplest. It was originally known as SSFDC, short for solid state floppy drive disk. SmartMedia cards contain only flash memory on a card without control circuits. The problem with this is that compatibility from different generations can call for manufacture upgrades. SmartMedia is now obsolete, the Solid State Floppy Disk Form originally masterminded the development of the SmartMedia standard.

    MultiMediaCard

    MMC (^) was coevolved by SanDisk and Infineon Technologies AG (formally called Siemens AG) in November 1997 for use in smart phones, MP3 players, digital cameras, and camcorders. The MMC used an easy 7-pin interface to devices and contains low-voltage flash memory. The MultiMediaCard Association was founded in 1998 to promote the MMC standard and aid development of new products. In November 2002, MMCA announced the development of the Reduced Size MultiMediaCard (RS-MMC), which minimizes the size of the standard MMC by 40% and can be adapted for use with standard MMC devices. The first flash memory cards in this form factor were introduced in early 2004 to support compact smartphones. In 2008, the NNCA merged with JEDEC which is the global leader in developing open standards for the microelectronics industry.

    Secure Digital

    A SecureDigital (SD) storage device is about the same size as he MMC (many devices can use both types of flash memory), but it’s a more sophisticated product. SD, which was codeveloped by Toshiba, Panasonic (Matsushita Electric) and SanDisk in 1999, gets its name from two certain features. The first is encrypted storage and the other is a mechanical write-protection switch. The SD slot can also be used to add memory to Palm PDAs. SDIO was created in January 2002 to enable SD cards to be used with small digital cameras and various other brands of PDAs and others. The SD Card Association was founded in 2000 to promote the SD standard and aid in the development of new products. Remember that some laptops have built-in SD slots. Reduced-size versions of SD include MiniSD (2003) and MicroSD (2005). MiniSD and MicroSD are popular choices for smartphones and can be adapted to a standard SD slot. MicrosSD is compatible with TransFlash standard for mobile phones. The original SD standard allowed for memory card capacities of up to 2GB. To support higher capacities the SDHC (High Capacity) standard was created in 2006. SDHC supports cards from 4GB to 32GB in capacity. To increase capacity beyond 32GB, the SDXC (eXtended Capacity) format was released in 2009. SDXC also supports SDHC and standard SD cards. A device that supports SDHC also accepts standard SD cards, but such a device does not support SDXC cards. Devices that support only standard either SDHC or SDXC cards.

    Sony Memory Stick

    Sony is often involved with laptop computers and a variety of digital cameras and camcorder products, has its own proprietary version of flash memory known as the Sony Memory Stick. This device features an erase-protection switch, which prevents accidental erasure of your photographs. Sony has also licensed Memory Stick technology to other companies, such as Lexar Media and SanDisk. Lexar announced the enhanced Memory Stick Pro in 2003. The Memory Stick Pro includes MagicGate encryption technology, which enables digital rights management, and Lexar’s proprietary high-speed memory controller. Memory stick Pro is sometimes referred to as MagicGate Memory Stick. The Memory Stick Pro Duo is smaller, and lighter than the standard Memory Stick Pro. It can also be adapted to devices designed for the Memory Stick Pro. Sony after that released “Mark 2” certified versions of the Memory Stick Pro in 2008. This certification indicated that the cards were suitable for use with AVCHDD (Advanced Video Coding High Definition) recording devices. Sony has as well released a smaller Memory Stick Micro (also called M2) format in 2006, which was designed to compete with microSD. In 2009 Sony introduced the Memory Stick XC (eXtended Capacity) format in order to compete with SDXC. Because of the memory stick formats are proprietary and only used in Sony devices, it is recommended to avoid them when possible. To avoid paying more and having to use hard to find proprietary memory. Sony however, has new devices that are including support for industry standard SD memory formats in response to the negative backlash against its proprietary Memory Stick.

    ATA Flash PC Card

    Despite the PC card (PCMCIA) form factor being used for everything from SCSI cards, network cards, game adapters, etc.… its original use was computer memory as the old PCMCIIA acronym indicated (Personal Computer Memory Card International Association). Unlike normal RAM modules, PC Card memory acts like a disk drive, using the PCMCIA ATA (AT Attachment) standard. PC cards come in different thicknesses (Type 1 is 3.3mm, Type 2 is 5mm, and Type 3 is 10.5mm) but all are 3.3 inch long by 2.13 wide. Type 1 and 2 cards are used for ATA-compliant hard disks, a type 3 can be used as two type 2 slots as well.

    xD-Picture Card

    On July 2002, Olympus and Fujifilm, the major supporters behind SmartMedia flash memory standard for digital cameras, announced the xD-Picture Card as much smaller and more durable replacement for SmartMedia. As well as being only one-third the size of SmartMedia (making it the smallest flash memory format yet) xD-Picture Card Media has a faster controller to enable faster image capture. Both 16MB and 32MB cards (commonly already in cameras) record data at speeds of 1.3MBps, where the 64MB and largest cards record data at 3MBps. The read speed for all sizes is 5MBps. The media is manufactured for Olympus and Fujifilm’s by Toshiba, and because xD-Picture media is optimized for the difference in cameras (Olympus’s media supports the panorama mode found in some Olympus xD-Picture cameras) you should buy the same brand as your digital camera. Just as with the proprietary Sony Memory Stick formats, it is generally recommended to avoid the proprietary xD-Picture format when you can and suggested to use industry standard memory formats such as SD. Because of the backlash against proprietary formats, Olympus and Fujifilm abounded xD-Picture card in 2010.

Today, SSDs are used for many task magnetic and optical drivers have done in the past, including system drives, primary and secondary data storage, and removable-media storage.

But increasing the clock speed of a serial connection is easier than increasing that of a parallel connection so that higher speeds are more offset the difference in the number of bits sent at a time. Serial interfaces can be designed to be faster than parallel interfaces and cheaper and having less complexity. With a serial us, data is sent one at time, right after the other. Because there is nothing to worry about having multiple bits arrive at the same time, clocking rate and increase majorly. Contrary wise, parallel buses tend to interfere with each other. Serial has once more proven to be the correct chose as only one or two have signal wires, so that crosstalk and interference between the wire and cable is insignificant.

  • Smart phones

  • WPAN (wireless PAN). Since there is no single device controlling or managing this type of network, PAN and WANs are examples of ad-hoc networks.
